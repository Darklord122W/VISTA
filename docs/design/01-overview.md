# The capacity wall, and what load shedding buys

*Every measured number on this page is **reported**: it comes from the paper's
campaign, whose data this repository does not distribute. They are here because
the argument needs magnitudes, not because you can re-derive them from a
checkout. To take your own, see
[`../reproduction/01-run-the-experiments.md`](../reproduction/01-run-the-experiments.md).*

This page is the conceptual entry point. It answers one question — *why does a
multi-camera pipeline need a scheduler at all?* — in vocabulary that does not
mention GStreamer. `02-deepstream-pipeline.md` then names every element.

---

## 1. The arithmetic that forces the problem

Take `N` cameras, each delivering a frame every `T` milliseconds, sharing one
batched detector that takes `S(K)` milliseconds to run a batch of `K` frames.

The system is oversubscribed whenever a full batch costs more than a frame
period. The paper writes the **load ratio**

```
rho = S / T
```

and the consequence is not a tuning problem, it is conservation of work: for
every second of video, a fraction

```
1 - 1/rho
```

of all arriving frames **cannot be processed**, on any schedule, with any
batching parameter, in any order. At `rho = 1.86` — the paper's primary
operating point — that is 46% of the input. The frames are not lost to a bug.
There is no GPU time for them.

Our rig instantiates `rho` four times over (paper Table I, generated by
`analysis/e1_figures.py`):

| detector | S(4) (ms) | capacity (f/s) | rho | regime |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| YOLO11n | 28.3 | 141 | **0.84** | below capacity |
| YOLO11s | 33.5 | 119 | **1.00** | exactly at capacity |
| YOLO11m | 62.4 | 64 | **1.86** | oversubscribed (primary) |
| YOLO11l | 78.3 | 51 | **2.33** | severely oversubscribed |

Arrival load is ≈119 f/s. That number is not a nominal figure; it is what the
replay front-end actually delivers, and it can be derived exactly:

```
4 cameras x (30 fps / 0.961 rate factor) x (44-2)/44 gap injection = 119.2 f/s
```

(The rate factor and gap injection are the replay's live-fidelity model;
`02-deepstream-pipeline.md` §4 explains both, and `--gap-every N` drops **two
consecutive** frames of every N — a detail that matters more than it looks,
because it is also how the Static-Decimation baseline is built.)

`rho = 119.2 / 64 = 1.86` reproduces Table I exactly.

## 2. Two outputs, one of them unmanaged

The observation the paper is built on: **an overloaded pipeline has two
outputs.** It emits processed frames, and it emits discarded frames. Every
serving system in the literature optimizes the first. The second is left to
whatever happens.

What happens, on a stock pipeline, is transport backpressure — and it makes
three decisions nobody chose:

1. **Which frames die.** The bounded capture ring at the top of the chain
   overwrites its **newest** arrivals when it fills. The freshest data is
   destroyed first; the stalest is faithfully served.
2. **Where they die.** In the kernel's capture ring — upstream of every
   userspace counter. The application's own coverage statistic reads
   `1.0000` while half the frames are gone. This is not hyperbole: on the
   paper's live rig the stock run's own log reported `processed 6433 of 6433
   arrived frames` on a run where ~14,000 frames were captured. It is telling
   the truth about what it saw.
3. **How stale the survivors are.** Frames queue behind the whole backlog, so
   staleness ≈ backlog ÷ throughput. The backlog is bounded by the buffer
   pool, so **output age is a transport configuration, not a property of the
   workload** — 857 ms at the deep replay pool, 410 ms at live pool depth,
   276 ms on the live rig. All three are the same policy.

The perverse end state: the operator sees full batches and healthy FPS, every
event is reported hundreds of milliseconds late, and no statistic anywhere says
which camera's frames died.

## 3. The funnel

Fig. 1 of the paper ("frame funnel") is the measurement of this. Read as a
funnel, at `rho = 1.86`:

```
  ~119 f/s arrive at the cameras
      |
      |  46% die in the capture ring        <- silent, newest-first, uncounted
      v
  ~54% reach the application
      |
      |  survivors queue behind the backlog
      v
  emitted 857 ms after capture              <- "processed", but far too late
```

Two properties make this the pathology rather than a cost:

- **The loss is at the top of the chain but caused at the bottom.** The GPU is
  the bottleneck; the frame dies in the kernel ring, the one place no
  application code can observe.
- **The knobs are inert.** Sweeping the batcher's waiting deadline across its
  full range changes neither outcome. Measured: an aggressive 5 ms deadline
  lands at 853 ms mean age against the default's 857 ms, and the transport's
  keep-newest option lands at 856 ms — all within noise of each other
  (the stock, 5 ms-deadline and keep-newest arms at YOLO11m, medians of 5).
  Under a
  permanent backlog every batch is full *before* the deadline timer starts
  counting, so the timer never fires. The decision is made before any batching
  parameter can act.

## 4. What load shedding buys

VISTA does not create capacity. At `rho = 1.86` it drops the same ~46% of
frames the stock pipeline drops. It changes **who decides, which frames, and
whether anyone is told**:

| | stock pipeline | VISTA |
|---|---|---|
| who decides | the kernel capture ring | the scheduler |
| which frame dies | the **newest** arrival | the **least valuable** (scored) |
| loss counted? | no (coverage reads 100%) | yes — `arrivals == admitted + policy_drops`, exactly |
| output age | a *configuration* (276–857 ms by pool depth) | bounded by construction (`tau_max` + service) |
| clock | free-running batcher deadline | inference completions |

The trade, at the primary operating point (the paper's Table II, quoted — the
runs behind it do not ship here):

| policy | coverage (%) | mean age (ms) | TTA@0.5 s (%) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Stock-Default | 46.2 | 857 | **0.0** |
| Stock-LiveDepth | 44.8 | 410 | 18.7 |
| **VISTA-Fresh** | 38.8 | **93** | **30.1** |

Six to seven points of coverage buy a 4.4x reduction in output age against the
live-calibrated baseline (9.2x against the deep-pool default), and move
half-second event awareness from 0% to 30%.

The Stock-Default row is the one to sit with. **Zero** of the 123 reference
events are reported within 500 ms of onset — not because the detector missed
them, but because no event can be reported younger than the standing queue.
An 857 ms queue cannot produce a 500 ms answer. That is a structural
statement, and it is why the paper's claim is about *awareness*, not accuracy.

## 5. Why this is a scheduling problem, not a filtering problem

Camera frames have a property that serving requests do not: they are
**perishable and replaceable**. A newer frame of the same view supersedes an
older one. A dropped request is a failure; a dropped frame is a frame whose
successor arrived 33 ms later carrying nearly the same information, only
fresher.

That is what makes shedding legitimate — and what makes *which* frame to shed a
real decision with a real value function, rather than an error path. Under the
capacity wall, dropping is the routine output. It deserves a policy, a
fairness rule, bounded state, and an audit trail.

The three things VISTA needs to provide that are, notably, not DeepStream-specific:

1. an interception point before the shared batcher,
2. an inference-completion signal,
3. a monotonic local clock.

Nothing depends on camera timestamps, camera synchronization, or the batcher's
internals. `06-local-clocks.md` explains why that third item says *local* — and
why it is a design commitment rather than a convenience.
